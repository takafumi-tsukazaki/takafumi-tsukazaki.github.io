Kalman Filter Visualizer — x̂ₜ

A state space model hides a state xₜ that evolves by a state equation xₜ = F·xₜ₋₁ + wₜ and is seen only through noisy observations yₜ = H·xₜ + vₜ. The Kalman filter recovers the state recursively: each step predicts from the model, then updates toward the new observation by the Kalman gain K. Drag the time cursor t₀ (left panel) to inspect any step; the right panel opens that step up as a tug-of-war between the model's prediction (prior) and the data (likelihood). Tune the process noise Q and observation noise R to watch the filter trust the model (small Q) or chase the data (large Q). Switch Panel A between the filter and the RTS smoother, and use the bottom panel to forecast beyond the data — where q = Q/R fixes the steady-state gain and the exponential-smoothing weight α.

We never see xₜ directly — only the noisy yₜ (dots). The filter line is x̂ₜ = E[xₜ | y₀ … yₜ]; the shaded band is its ±2σ uncertainty. Smaller Q ties the estimate to the model (smoother); larger Q lets it chase the data.

Predict, then update
predict: x⁻ = F·x̂ , P⁻ = F·P·F + Q
update: K = P⁻ / (P⁻ + R) , x̂ = x⁻ + K·(y − x⁻) , P = (1 − K)·P⁻
The gain K ∈ [0, 1] is the trust dial: K → 0 keeps the prediction (trust the model), K → 1 jumps to the observation (trust the data). K rises when the prediction is uncertain (large P⁻) or the sensor is clean (small R).

Steady state & exponential smoothing
forecast: x̂(T+h) = Fʰ·x̂(T) , P(T+h) = F·P(T+h−1)·F + Q (no data → cone widens)
local level: x̂ₜ = (1 − α)·x̂ₜ₋₁ + α·yₜ , α = K∞
With F = 1 the prediction variance grows without bound (P + h·Q), so the forecast cone keeps widening. The steady-state Kalman gain K∞ solves the Riccati fixed point and equals the EWMA weight α: small q = Q/R ⇒ small α ⇒ heavy smoothing that trusts the model; large q ⇒ α → 1 ⇒ the estimate chases each observation. For AR(1) (F < 1) the forecast reverts toward the mean and the cone plateaus.

Trend forecast
x̂(T+h) = Fʰ·x̂(T) ⇒ level ≈ μ̂(T) + h·β̂(T) (straight line along the slope)
P(T+h) = F·P(T+h−1)·F + Q , Q = diag(Q_level, Q_slope)
Because the level integrates the slope, slope uncertainty feeds into the level variance and the cone grows roughly like h³ — far faster than the local level's linear growth. Small Q_slope keeps the forecast a stiff straight line; large Q_slope lets the slope drift, fanning the cone open. The white dashed line is what actually happened.
